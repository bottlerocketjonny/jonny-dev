# jonny-dev — Design Context

A single-page Astro portfolio for **Jonny Coddington**, a Sheffield-based full-stack developer (Java / Spring Boot / React / TypeScript) with eleven years making records before moving into software. The site introduces him, lists a handful of projects (NHS chatbots, festival recycling, a grassroots music booking platform, a security pipeline), points to open-source contributions, and provides ways to get in touch.

## Design Context

### Users

Ranked by priority:

1. **Engineering hiring managers / eng leads** — senior ICs and tech leads evaluating whether Jonny could join their team. They skim fast but read carefully when something earns their attention. They've seen a thousand dev portfolios.
2. **Recruiters doing first-pass screens** — need to extract role fit, location, stack, and contact details in under thirty seconds.
3. **Potential clients / collaborators** — for Japeto Labs work and the Simple venue-booking project. Want to judge credibility and reach.
4. **Fellow developers / peers** — tertiary audience; not designed for.

Typical context: desk-based browsing during work hours on a laptop, with the occasional phone glance from a recruiter or a peer share. Not a late-night / dim-room product.

### Job to be done

When someone lands on this page, they should walk away with **two feelings, in this order**:

1. *I'd hire this person.* Credible track record, serious stack, shipped work with real stakes (NHS trusts, festival crowds, paying bands).
2. *This person looks interesting and different.* They write carefully. They care about craft. They have a life outside the stack.

The second is what converts a maybe into a reply.

### Brand personality

Three words: **editorial, understated, literate.**

- Editorial, not product. The voice belongs to a magazine column or a liner note, not a SaaS landing page. Full sentences over bullet point soup. Restraint over abundance.
- Understated. Quiet confidence. The work carries itself — no loud adjectives, no "revolutionary", no award-shelf name-dropping.
- Literate. Copy is considered. Italics do real emotional work. Line breaks are intentional.

Emotional register: **calm confidence and warmth, with something rewarding under the surface.** Not cold minimalism. A little human. The music background can sit in the margins (one-line nod in the intro) rather than a whole section — the fact of it reads, the prose around it doesn't need to. The page should feel *alive* to navigate — not boring, not a scroll-until-footer read. There's a considered layer of interaction to discover without asking the visitor to hunt for it.

### Anti-references

Do not look like:

- **A SaaS landing page.** No hero metrics, no gradient buttons, no "Trusted by" logo walls, no feature-card grids with matching icons, no testimonial carousels.
- **A generic dev portfolio.** No terminal-green monospace everywhere, no ASCII self-portrait, no "hello.world()" jokes, no `const jonny = { ... }` snippets, no GitHub-contribution heatmaps as wallpaper.
- **A Vercel/Linear clone.** No dark-with-glowing-accents default, no purple-to-blue gradients, no aurora backgrounds, no "get started" CTAs.
- **A design-agency site.** No oversized kerned type covering the viewport, no marquee tickers, no unreadable-but-beautiful experimental layouts that block the job-to-be-done.

The site should make an engineering hiring manager pause and think *where did this come from* — not *which template is this.*

### Aesthetic direction

The existing direction is correct and should be carried forward. Nothing is sacred, but the current choices are good; revise only with reason.

- **Palette.** Tinted neutrals (warm petrol-leaning off-whites and inks) with two accents: petrol `#5a8599` for interactive / structural, dusty copper `#c87d4a` for editorial emphasis (italic words, pullquote rule). Rule: petrol never does emotion; copper never does affordance.
- **Type.** Instrument Serif for display and italics (the emotional layer). Inter Tight Variable for body. JetBrains Mono for tiny labels, years, and eyebrows. No additional families without a strong reason.
- **Theme.** Light default. Dark available via `ThemeToggle`. Both pass contrast. Dark mode drops the background glow.
- **Layout.** Single 680px column, generous vertical rhythm, subtle grain overlay, dual-tone petrol/copper corner glow in light mode.
- **Motion.** Restrained. Fade-ups on load, an underline draw on the hero accent, a nudging arrow on project hover. Respect `prefers-reduced-motion` (already wired).

### Design principles

1. **Editorial before technical.** If a section could be a paragraph of prose instead of a card grid, make it prose. Italics, pullquotes, and line breaks carry meaning — use them instead of icons, chips, or labels.
2. **Warmth through restraint.** The copper italic does the emotional work so body copy stays quiet. Adding more warm surfaces, more gradients, or more "friendly" imagery makes it feel less warm, not more.
3. **Differentiation by craft, not volume.** Standing out means a considered detail someone notices on the second visit — a well-set pullquote, a typographic lockup, a hover that feels physical. It does not mean louder headings or bigger images.
4. **Delight through interaction, not decoration.** Navigation should reward attention. The site is currently a scroll-and-read; it shouldn't be. But "fun" here means intentional interaction (keyboard shortcuts, considered hover reveals, section-by-section movement, one small surprise) — not particle systems, scroll-jacking, or cursor trails. The brand register still holds: restrained, editorial, warm. If a visitor never discovers the interactive layer, the page still reads perfectly; if they do, it earns a second look.
5. **Low-maintenance surfaces.** Any new section must be either content-light (auto-populated from a collection, a feed, a commit log, a "now" snippet) or capped to a few sentences Jonny will actually keep up to date. No empty blogs, no stale "reading" lists.
6. **WCAG AA is the floor, not the ceiling.** Contrast, focus rings, keyboard paths, motion fallbacks — all non-negotiable. Verify both themes. Current copper focus ring at 2px / 2px offset is the pattern to match. Any interactive layer added for "fun" must also be reachable from the keyboard and respect `prefers-reduced-motion`.
7. **Single-page discipline.** The site is one page and should stay that way unless a second surface genuinely earns its URL. Additions should slot into the existing vertical flow before they get their own route.

### Open thread

Two connected problems to solve in future `/impeccable craft` sessions:

**A. The page "needs something else"** but Jonny doesn't want to write much to support it. Low-to-high effort candidates:

- A **"currently"** one-liner (3–5 words, updated rarely) — what he's listening to, reading, or building this week.
- A **signature / mark / monogram** in the footer or header margin — a small drawn detail that lives where a logo would.
- A **colophon** — a short footer note on how the site is built and what it's set in (the kind of thing a magazine does on its last page). Written once, never touched.
- A **changelog / notes** section auto-populated from git tags or a single markdown file, surfaced inline rather than as its own page.
- A **subtle generative element** tied to time of day or scroll — one considered motif, not a particle system.

**B. The page is too scroll-and-read; it should be rewarding to navigate.** Candidates that fit the editorial register (not SaaS, not terminal pastiche):

- **Keyboard navigation** — press `1`–`4` to jump to a project, `j`/`k` or arrow keys to step through sections, `?` to reveal the shortcut list. Magazine-style "page turning" without a command palette.
- **Section-as-page feel** — scroll-snap or a section stepper so each piece of work feels like turning a page rather than continuous scroll. Still works as a normal scroll for anyone who wants that.
- **Considered hover reveals** — hovering a project surfaces a secondary layer: a pullquote, a small detail photo, a link to the live thing. Current hover only nudges the arrow; it could do more emotional work.
- **An accent-word easter egg** — clicking or hovering the copper italic in the hero cycles through a small set of alternate phrases (the "quiet interfaces" lockup becomes "stubborn details", "quiet infrastructure", etc.). One tiny discoverable moment.
- **A cursor affordance** with meaning — a soft custom cursor or section-specific label that hints at what's clickable, in the magazine sense (not a ring-follower).
- **A keyboard-driven project peek** — pressing a number key opens an inline detail view for that project without a route change, closed with `Esc`. Content-light (uses existing markdown bodies).
- **A time-aware greeting** or subtle surface change — the hero eyebrow reads differently before 10am, after 6pm, or on the day a project went live. Near-zero maintenance, rewards a second visit.

Several of these could combine (e.g. keyboard nav + scroll-snap + inline project peek is one coherent interaction model). None are decided. They're candidates to evaluate when the time comes, each subject to the WCAG AA and reduced-motion rules above.
